Introduction

"The body is cleansed by water; the mind is purified by truthfulness; the essence of personality (atman) is purified by wisdom and austerities; and intelligence (buddhi) by higher knowledge". (Manu-Smriti (V. 109))-the most important and earliest work on law, ethics and conduct.

Impurified mind can never grasp the transparency and luminosity of the Self. From the "Maitrayaniya-Upanishad (VI. 34) we know "â€¦that the mind is said twofold: pure and impure. It is impure from contact with desires; pure when free from desire. When one has liberated the mind from slothfulness and heedlessness and made immovable and dissolved, this is the supreme estate. He whose mind has become pure through absorption and entered the Self, experiences a bliss impossible to describe in words and only ineligible to the inner instrument." Unenlightened individuals identify with "their" mind in the same way in which they identify with "their" body. In their everyday life, they rarely merely witness the activities of the body-mind. That is, they seldom identify with the Spirit or Self, which transcends all bodily and mental states.

As spiritual practitioners make progress on the path, they learn to disentangle themselves from their psychosomatic reactions. Increasingly, they position themselves in the transcendental witness, the Self. They are increasingly present as that Self. They can free themselves from deep-rooted notion of "I" and "mine", they can attain the virtue of contentment. This is a pervasive mood, which reflects the stillness of transcendental Self. Without contentment, or elated serenity, success on the spiritual path is unthinkable. Only positive, settled frame of mind is conductive to inner strength and attainment of higher states of consciousness.

Equanimity also stands for that state of mind in which one remains unaffected by sorrow and pleasure, success and failure, praise and blame, ups and downs of life. On the highest level, it is the all-embracing vision of sameness, which reveals to adepts the ultimate Being wherever their glance may fall. In the words of Bhagavad-Gita (VI, 29): "He whose self is yoked in Yoga and who everywhere beholds the same, sees the Self abiding in all beings and all beings in the Self."

True practitioners are content with what comes to them from life. The more they eradicate their self-will (ahamkara) and the more their mind participates in the tranquillity of the supramundane Reality, the less are their attachments to earthly joys and pleasures. The bliss of the Self is unexcelled and in comparison with it the pleasures of the world seem vain.

For the ascetic, ordinary life is unnatural because it ignores our true identity, which is Self.
"Knowledge springs from sorrow". (Markandeya-Purana (XXXIX. 2) and Mahabharata (III, 2. 24)) "He who knows Self, traverses sorrow". (Chandogya-Upanishad (VII. I. 3))
