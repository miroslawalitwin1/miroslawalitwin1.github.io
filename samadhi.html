Samadhi

The first steps leading to Samadhi are one pointed concentration, meditation, and contemplation. Samadhi may be defined as a process of diving into deeper layers of oneâ€™s consciousness, which functions through different grades of mind. Consciousness is an aspect of the Ultimate Reality in manifestation, and its expression depends upon particular grade of mind through which it is functioning, the coarser the medium the more limited the expression.

The first aspect of Samadhi is the distinction between Samprajnata and Asamprajnata Samadhi. The word Prajna stands for higher consciousness. The distinctive characteristic of this higher consciousness which unfolds in Samadhi is that the mind is cut off completely from physical world and the consciousness is centered in one or other vehicle beginning with the lower mental body and ending with the Atmic vehicle. The consciousness is thus free from the burden and interference of the physical brain.

In Samprajnata Samadhi there is a "seed" in the field of consciousness and the consciousness is fully directed in it. This seed may be of any kind and may exist on any plane of mind. A mental image of child, a concept of mathematical principle, an all-embracing vision of the Unity of life are all seeds of different kind and belong to different planes.

In Asamprajnata Samadhi there is no "seed" and therefore there is nothing to draw the consciousness outwards and hold it there. So as soon as seed is dropped or suppressed the consciousness begins to recede automatically to its centre and is in the state of void until it emerges into the next subtler vehicle and appear as a seed of the next higher plane, and the direction of consciousness again becomes from centre outwards.

In the diagram are shown the different stages of Samprajna and Asamprajna Samadhi and their correspondences to different vehicles. When they are passed, the Nirbija Samadhi is practised to remove the final and the subtlest veil covering Reality. Then the light of consciousness can be seen as it really is, and the consciousness of the Purusha/Spirit emerges.

The Science of Yoga cannot be mastered in one life but only in succession of strenuous lives devoted exclusively to Yogic ideal. While the beginner may remain entangled on the lower planes for a considerable time extending years, the advanced Yogi can transfer his consciousness from one plane to another with lightning rapidity, and in case of the Adept all the planes really merge into one, because the passage up or down is so swift and easy that it is a matter of focusing consciousness on this or that vehicle.
