Golden Ratio

The first clear definition of Golden Ratio was given around 300 B.C. by the founder of geometry, Euclides of Alexandria. He did not really discover geometry. It was known long before him. When line AB is certainly longer than the segment AC; at the same time , the segment AC is longer than BC then the ratio of the length of AC to that CB is the same as the ratio of AB to AC - then the line has been cut in extreme and mean ratio, or in a Golden Ratio.

This is irrational number, never ending, never repeating and is equal 1.618033987...

This number is hidden in spiral patterns of Nature starting from galaxies, pineaples conical shapes, spiral patterns in the sunflower formed by florets, the rose petals, the mollusc shells, or flight of peregrine falcons when attacking their prey.

Observations conducted with the Hubble Space Telescope also revealed that there are many spiral galaxies in some one hundred billion galaxies in our observable universe. They also obey the rule of logarithmic spiral hidden in the Golden Rectangle and Golden Triangle as well.

Golden Ratio can be found not only in natural phenomena but also in a variety of human-made objects and works of art. For example, in Salvadore Dali's painting "Sacrament of the Last Supper", where dimensions of the painting are in Golden Ratio to each other (about 105x65 inches). Part of dodecahedron is seen in the picture floating above the table. Regular solids that can be precisely enclosed by a sphere, and dodecahedron in particular, are intimately related to the Golden Ratio.
